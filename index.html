🌿 Hampton, NH Garden Guide

Complete care reference — flowers, tomato, oregano

🌸
Wild Columbine
Trim: Late June – Early July, after blooms die
Cut stems
Cut each flower stem down to the base where it meets the leafy mound. Leave all the leaves alone — they feed the plant roots for next year.
💡 Rozanne Cranesbill in the same pot will spread and hide the columbine's bare stems naturally.
💜
Rozanne Cranesbill Geranium
Trim: Midsummer (July) when leggy or tired looking
Cut by half
Cut the entire plant back by half — every stem. This is called the "Chelsea chop." It feels drastic but this plant loves it.
💡 Bounces back fuller within 2 weeks and blooms again through frost.
🤍
White Phlox
Trim: As individual flower clusters fade
Deadhead only
Only remove individual dead flower clusters — snip just below the brown spent head. Do not cut the whole plant down.
💡 Check every few days. Violas in the same pot will fade in July heat — just pull them out and leave phlox solo.
🐱
Catnip
Trim: After each bloom flush — 2-3x per season
Cut by half
Cut the entire plant down by half after the first bloom spike turns brown. Every stem. Forces fresh growth and repeat blooming.
💡 You can get 2–3 bloom cycles per season from catnip just by cutting it back each time.

Storm Damage Recovery
Do this now — within the first week after storm
Act now
Step 1 — Remove hanging branches: Any branch hanging by a thread cannot transport water. Cut cleanly at the base where it meets the main stem. Don't leave stubs.

Step 2 — Remove painters tape: Once damaged branches are cut, remove the splints. They're no longer needed.

Step 3 — Do NOT fertilize yet: Wait 5–7 days after the storm. Fertilizing a stressed plant makes it worse.

Step 4 — Check for suckers: Stressed plants push extra suckers as a survival response. Pinch any small ones in the V crotches now.
⚠️ The yellowing leaves you see are normal post-storm stress. They should green up within 1–2 weeks once you fertilize and the plant stabilizes.
💡 Good sign: if you see small new growth points near the center, the plant is actively recovering. Keep it in full sun.
🌱
Sucker Removal
Check every 4–5 days all season
Pinch or snip
Suckers grow in the V-shaped crotch between the main stem and a branch. They steal energy from fruit production.

Under 2 inches: pinch off with your fingers
Over 2 inches: use clean scissors
Never remove more than ⅓ of the plant at once
💡 Check every 4–5 days in warm weather — they grow fast. Beefsteaks especially need regular suckering to produce big heavy fruit.
💧
Tomato Watering
Daily check in summer heat
Consistent = key
How to check: Stick your finger 2 inches into the soil. Dry at that depth = water now. Still damp = wait.

How to water: Pour slowly at the base of the plant until water comes out the drainage holes. Don't rush it.

Frequency guide:
— Cool/cloudy days: every 2–3 days
— Hot sunny NH summer days: possibly daily
— After heavy rain: skip — check soil first
⚠️ Inconsistent watering (dry then soaked then dry) causes blossom end rot — black bottoms on tomatoes. Keep it steady.
💡 Water in the morning if possible. Wet leaves overnight invites fungal issues.
🌾
Tomato Fertilizing Schedule
Pennington Rejuvenate — all season plan
Every 4–6 weeks
May Mix 1–2 tbsp Rejuvenate into soil at planting. Water in well. Skip if plant is storm-stressed — wait 5–7 days first.
June Sprinkle Rejuvenate on top of soil, water in. This is when the plant starts really growing — nitrogen helps green up leaves.
July When you see first flowers forming, switch focus to phosphorus. Add a handful of bone meal mixed into the top soil once. Continue Rejuvenate every 4–6 weeks.
Aug Keep fertilizing lightly. Fruit is sizing up now. Don't over-fertilize or you get big leaves, small tomatoes.
💡 You're in NH — your season ends around late September/first frost. Push for fruit by early August for best results.
🍃
Harvesting Oregano
Start now — harvest regularly all season
Snip outer stems
How to harvest: Snip stems from the outer edges of the plant, cutting just above a leaf node (where two leaves branch off the stem). Use scissors not pulling.

How much to take: Never more than ⅓ of the plant at once. The more you harvest, the bushier it grows.

Using fresh vs dried: Fresh Italian oregano is about twice as strong as dried. If a recipe calls for 1 tsp dried, use ½ tsp fresh.
💡 Regular harvesting is actually better for the plant than leaving it alone — it prevents it from going to flower (bolting) which makes the leaves bitter.

Oregano Trimming, Height & Bolting

Keep under 4" · Trim every 3–4 weeks

Target height: 4 inches. Italian oregano in a container is healthiest and most productive kept at or under 4 inches tall. Once it creeps past that, stems get woody, airflow drops, and it's more likely to bolt.

📏 Height Guide — Where to Cut
  ↑  6–8"  ← TOO TALL — cut now, before bolting
  |   🌸   ← flower buds forming at tips (pinch these!)
  |   🌿
  ↓  4"    ← IDEAL HEIGHT — cut back to here
  ✂ ─────────────────────  ← cut line
  |   🌿   ← leave these lower leaves alone
  |   🌿
  ●         ← soil line

Cut just above a leaf node (where two leaves branch off). Never cut into bare woody stem with no leaves below.

🌸 Spotting a Bolt — Pinch These Immediately
  NORMAL STEM         BOLTING STEM
      🌿                  🌸 ← pinch here before this opens!
      |                   |
      🌿                  🌿
      |                   |
      🌿                  🌿

Bolting tip looks elongated and pointy with tiny buds. Normal leaf tips are flat and rounded. Catch it early — once flowers fully open, nearby leaves turn bitter.

How often to trim:
  • Every 3–4 weeks through the growing season (June–Sept)
  • Any time it hits 6"+ — don't wait for the schedule
  • Any time you see flower buds — pinch immediately regardless of height
How much to cut:
  • Cut back to 4 inches from soil — all stems, not just the tall ones
  • Never remove more than ⅓ of the plant at once
  • Always cut just above a leaf node — never into bare woody stem
💡 The trimmings you cut are ready to use fresh or dry. Tie a small bundle and hang upside down for 1–2 weeks for dried oregano.
⚠️ If oregano bolts and you didn't catch it in time — cut the whole plant back hard to 2–3 inches, water well, and it will regrow fresh non-bitter leaves within 2 weeks.
May Plant everything. Water in well. Remove storm-damaged tomato branches. Start pinching oregano regularly. Columbine and phlox blooming.
June Fertilize tomato. Deadhead phlox regularly. Pinch tomato suckers every 4–5 days. Catnip first bloom — cut back by half when done. Violas fading in heat — pull when done.
July Tomato flowers forming — add bone meal once. Cut Rozanne back by half (Chelsea chop). Cut columbine stems to base. Catnip second bloom. Oregano watch for bolting.
Aug Tomatoes sizing up — keep watering consistently. Catnip possible third bloom. Rozanne reblooming after chop. Light fertilizing only.
Sept Harvest tomatoes before first frost (late Sept in Hampton). Rozanne and catnip still going. Enjoy the garden before you head back to school!
